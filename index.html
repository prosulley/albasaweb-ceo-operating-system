The startup CEO's one true job

Make sure ALBASA WEB does not run out of money, clients, or momentum.

Everything else — the code, the designs, the server configs — flows from that. Your PMP and operations background already wires you for this. Now apply it to directing a company, not a project.

Your three irreplaceable CEO functions

Set direction — and repeat it until everyone is tired of hearing it

You decide the sectors ALBASA targets, the services you offer, the clients you pursue, and the ones you decline. Every quarter, you produce a one-page company direction memo (not a slide deck — a memo). "This quarter we are closing 3 law firm retainers and building Siri Stores v2." Write it. Share it. Revisit it weekly.

Control cash — know your runway at all times

As CEO you must always know: how much cash is in the account, what is owed to you (receivables), what you owe others (payables), and how many months you can survive with zero new revenue. That number is your runway. If it drops below 2 months — that is a CEO emergency, not a Mubarak problem.

Own the client relationship — from discovery to renewal

You run every discovery call. You write or approve every proposal. You sign every contract. You handle escalations. You make the renewal call at month 5 of a 6-month retainer. Mubarak builds excellent things. You make sure a human being on the other end values and pays for those things.

What you do NOT do as CEO (delegate or drop)

Write code on client projects

Exception: internal tools for ALBASA itself. Once you have a developer, your hands leave client code. If you are both selling and building, you will do neither well.

Make technical architecture decisions

Which database, which hosting, which framework — Mubarak decides. You ask one question: "Can we deliver it in the timeline we promised the client?" If yes, trust the CTO. That is his domain.

Answer every WhatsApp message yourself

Set up WhatsApp Business auto-replies. Route inquiries to a Google Form intake. Then triage — only you respond to clients already in contract or high-value prospects. Everything else goes to a template or your future BD rep.

The CEO's non-negotiables — protect these blocks

Deep work before 12pm every day

Your highest-value thinking — proposals, strategy, client communication — happens in the morning. Never schedule admin, errands, or reactive tasks before noon. Protect this block like a contract deadline.

Monday 8am founder sync

30 minutes. Standing meeting. Never cancelled. This is your company's weekly nervous system. Without it, you and Mubarak operate in silos.

Friday financial check — 15 minutes minimum

Every Friday: open your Wave Accounting, check balances, chase one unpaid invoice, note MRR vs last week. Ignorance of your own finances is not busyness — it is a risk to the company.

The CEO decision framework — how to make fast, right calls

The ALBASA Decision Filter — ask these 4 questions in order

1. Does this move us closer to signed revenue or a retainer?
Yes → prioritise
No → schedule or drop
2. Is this reversible?
Yes → decide fast, move on
No → take 24 hours, consult Mubarak, then decide
3. Does this require Mubarak's technical input?
Yes → ask him one specific question, get a yes/no, then you decide
No → decide alone and inform him
4. Am I deciding this because it feels urgent or because it is actually important?
Urgent-only → park in Notion, revisit Friday
Important → decide now
Decision ownership matrix — who decides what

CEO decides alone (no consultation needed)

Pricing of any proposal. Which clients to pursue. Which clients to decline. Hiring decisions. Contract terms. Payment policy. Public communications (what you post on LinkedIn, your website). Budget allocation within agreed monthly limits.

CEO + CTO decide together (discuss, then CEO has final call)

Taking on a new type of project you've never done before. Committing to a delivery timeline on a large project. Hiring a developer. Pivoting a product feature. Entering a new sector. Signing a client with unusual or risky requirements.

CTO decides alone (inform CEO after)

Technology stack on any project. Hosting and infrastructure choices. Code architecture. Development tools and libraries. Security implementation approach. Which bugs are critical vs. nice-to-fix.

How to handle client scope creep (your most common decision)

The scope creep response — say this exact thing

When a client asks for something outside the original scope: "That's a great addition — it's not included in our current agreement. I'll put together a quick change order for your review. It typically adds [X days] and [GHC Y]. Want me to proceed?" Never say yes or no in the moment. Always put it in writing. Your PMP training makes this natural — apply the change control process you already know.

Your quarterly CEO review ritual — 2 hours every 90 days

The quarterly 6-question review

Sit alone for 2 hours every 90 days and answer: (1) What was our revenue this quarter vs last? (2) Which clients renewed and which left — and why? (3) Which sector gave us the best return on time? (4) What did we build this quarter that we can reuse next quarter? (5) What decision from 90 days ago now looks wrong? (6) What should we stop doing entirely? Write it in Notion. Share with Mubarak. Update your one-page company direction memo based on the answers.

The co-founder operating agreement — not a legal document, a working agreement

Two founders with no structure will eventually step on each other's decisions, create conflicting messages to clients, and build resentment silently. This prevents that.

Write down who owns what — today, in a Notion page titled "ALBASA Operating Agreement"

Suleman owns: client relationships, proposals, pricing, contracts, company finances, hiring, public positioning, project delivery (schedule and scope). Mubarak owns: technical architecture, code quality, security decisions, developer management, cybersecurity delivery, tool and stack choices. Both own: strategy, culture, major pivots, equity decisions.

Weekly founder sync — 30 minutes, Monday 8am, non-negotiable

Fixed agenda, always the same: (1) What did each of you do last week? 5 bullets each. (2) What is each blocked on? (3) What decisions need joint input this week? (4) What is the one priority each of you commits to completing before next Monday? Document it in a running Notion page. This becomes your company journal.

Monthly founder retrospective — 1 hour, last Friday of each month

Separate from the weekly sync. Bigger questions: Are we still aligned on where ALBASA is going? Is the workload distribution fair? Is there anything unsaid that needs to be said? Are we both compensating ourselves fairly? This is the meeting that prevents founder breakdowns before they happen.

Disagreement protocol — when you and Mubarak cannot agree

Step 1: each writes their position in 3 bullets (no verbal debates — writing forces clarity). Step 2: CEO makes the final call on business decisions, CTO on technical ones. Step 3: you trial the decision for 30 days, then review together. No grudges. No "I told you so." The decision was made with the information available.

Compensation — the conversation most founders avoid

Pay yourselves a fixed salary from Month 3 onward — even if it's small

Founders who don't pay themselves create invisible debt and resentment. Agree on a minimum founder salary (e.g. GHC 3,000/month each) as soon as MRR exceeds GHC 10,000. Increase it quarterly as revenue allows. Any profit above operating costs gets split per your equity agreement. Do not live off client project payments directly — that destroys your cash flow visibility.

CEO traps that kill lean startups — and how to avoid them

Trap 1 — Being the best developer in your own company

If you, Suleman, are still writing client code six months from now, your company has not grown — it has just gotten busier. Every hour you code is an hour you are not closing a GHC 25,000 contract. Your ceiling as a coder is your salary. Your ceiling as a CEO who builds a team is unlimited. Choose the ceiling.

Trap 2 — Saying yes to every client

In Ghana's referral-based market, you will be tempted to take any project that offers money. The wrong clients cost more than they pay — they erode your team, delay your real clients, and produce bad case studies. Decline clients who: won't pay 50% upfront, have no clear brief, want to negotiate every invoice, or whose project is outside your sector focus without a premium fee.

Trap 3 — Confusing busyness with progress

A full calendar and a full to-do list are not signs of a healthy startup — they are often signs that the CEO has failed to prioritise. Every Friday, ask: "Did I move the company closer to revenue today, or did I manage tasks?" If it was mostly tasks, Monday needs to be different.

Trap 4 — Avoiding hard conversations with clients

Ghanaian business culture prizes relationships — but relationships do not survive unspoken resentments. If a client is late on payment, call them on day 8, not day 40. If a project is going off-scope, raise a change order immediately. The earlier the hard conversation, the cheaper the resolution. Avoidance is not kindness — it is debt accumulation.

Trap 5 — No systems, all memory

If ALBASA WEB's knowledge lives only in your and Mubarak's heads, you are both irreplaceable — and that is a fragility, not a strength. Every process you do more than twice must be documented in Notion: client onboarding, proposal writing, invoicing, project kickoff, retainer renewal. "We don't have time to document" means "we plan to repeat every mistake." You, as CEO, model this — if you document, your team will document.

Trap 6 — Waiting until you feel "ready"

You will never feel ready to raise your prices, decline a bad client, hire your first employee, or pitch a large company. You gather information, make the best decision possible, execute it, and learn from the result. Your PMP methodology is exactly this — you do not wait for a perfect risk register before starting a project. You manage risk in motion.

The CEO's one daily question — write it somewhere you see it every morning

"What is the single most important thing I can do today that will make everything else easier or unnecessary?"

Do that first. Then do everything else. This one habit separates CEOs who build companies from founders who stay self-employed.
